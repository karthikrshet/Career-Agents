# Offer Comparison

# Overview

Offer Comparison is a decision operating system for candidates choosing between job, internship, remote, startup, enterprise, or campus placement offers. It prevents candidates from choosing only by headline salary, brand name, urgency, fear, or family pressure. The workflow converts offers into comparable data, defines candidate priorities, scores trade-offs, resolves unknowns, and produces a clear decision memo.

The best offer is not always the highest-paying one. It is the offer that best fits the candidate's financial needs, learning goals, role scope, manager quality, location constraints, risk tolerance, and long-term career direction. This workflow makes those factors visible.

# Target Audience

- Candidates choosing between two or more offers.
- Freshers comparing campus placement offers.
- Internship candidates comparing stipend, learning, and conversion potential.
- Developers choosing between startup, enterprise, remote, or product-company roles.
- Candidates deciding whether to accept, negotiate, delay, or decline.
- Contributors documenting offer-stage career systems.

# Prerequisites

- Written offer details for each opportunity.
- Role descriptions and expected responsibilities.
- Compensation components and benefits.
- Response deadlines.
- Candidate priorities and constraints.
- Notes from interviews about manager, team, culture, and growth.
- Clarification questions for missing information.

# Step-by-Step Execution Plan

1. **Create one offer profile per company.** Capture compensation, role, level, team, manager, location, work mode, benefits, probation, bond, start date, promotion path, and deadline.

2. **Mark unknowns.** Do not compare incomplete offers as if they are complete. Unknown equity, vague role scope, unclear manager, and hidden location rules must be clarified.

3. **Define decision criteria.** Choose criteria before scoring: compensation, learning, role fit, manager, culture, company stability, location, flexibility, growth, brand, risk, and personal constraints.

4. **Weight the criteria.** Not every factor matters equally. A candidate with financial pressure may weight cash higher. A fresher may weight learning and role quality higher. A remote candidate may weight timezone and flexibility heavily.

5. **Score each offer.** Use a 1-5 scale with notes. Scores without reasoning are not useful. Explain why each offer earns the score.

6. **Run clarification round.** Ask recruiters or hiring managers specific questions before final scoring. Example: "What does success look like in the first 90 days?" or "Who will I report to directly?"

7. **Analyze risks.** Identify downside scenarios: poor manager, narrow scope, unstable company, low learning, commute strain, compensation resentment, weak onboarding, or unclear expectations.

8. **Run regret pre-mortem.** Imagine six months into each offer. Ask why the candidate might regret choosing it. Add mitigation or mark risk as accepted.

9. **Check negotiation potential.** Use `salary-negotiation-coach` if one offer is strong except for compensation or terms.

10. **Write the decision memo.** State recommended offer, top reasons, trade-offs accepted, risks, and next message.

11. **Communicate professionally.** Accept one offer clearly, decline others respectfully, and keep relationships intact.

# Weekly Action Plan

**Week 1: Data Collection**

- Gather written offers.
- Build offer profiles.
- Identify missing terms.
- Ask clarification questions.

**Week 2: Criteria and Scoring**

- Define priorities.
- Assign weights.
- Score offers.
- Review emotional reactions separately from facts.

**Week 3: Risk and Negotiation**

- Run regret pre-mortem.
- Identify negotiable gaps.
- Negotiate if appropriate.
- Update scores after revised terms.

**Week 4: Decision and Closure**

- Write decision memo.
- Accept chosen offer.
- Decline alternatives politely.
- Prepare onboarding success plan.

If offer deadlines are shorter, compress the sequence into days, but do not skip criteria and unknowns.

# Daily Checklist

- Review one offer for missing terms.
- Update comparison matrix.
- Ask one clarification question if needed.
- Recheck priorities against scores.
- Separate external pressure from personal criteria.
- Document recruiter replies.
- Draft or revise decision memo.
- Avoid accepting before written terms are clear.

# KPIs / Success Metrics

- Percentage of critical terms clarified.
- Decision criteria completeness.
- Priority alignment score.
- Risk visibility score.
- Negotiation improvement if attempted.
- Confidence in final decision.
- Ability to explain choice in one paragraph.
- Timely acceptance and decline communication.

# Common Mistakes

- Choosing only by salary.
- Choosing only by brand.
- Ignoring manager and team quality.
- Comparing offers with missing information.
- Letting family, peers, or urgency override stated priorities.
- Forgetting location, commute, timezone, or visa constraints.
- Underestimating role scope and learning trajectory.
- Declining poorly and damaging relationships.

# Expected Outcomes

By the end of this workflow, the candidate should have complete offer profiles, a weighted decision matrix, clarification answers, risk analysis, negotiation notes, and a decision memo. The candidate should understand not only which offer to choose, but what trade-offs they are accepting.

The workflow succeeds when the candidate can say: "I chose this offer because it best matches my priorities, I understand the risks, and I know what I am giving up."
